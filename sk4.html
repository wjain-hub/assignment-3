SK 4 Phonosuper

The Braun SK 4 Phonograph, designed by Dieter Rams and Hans Gugelot in 1956, revolutionized audio equipment with its minimalist aesthetic and innovative use of materials. Unlike traditional wooden radio enclosures, the SK 4 introduced a sleek, modern design with a white metal body and a transparent plexiglass lid—a groundbreaking choice that earned it the nickname "Snow White's Coffin."

Design and Innovation

Material Innovation: First audio product to feature a clear plexiglass lid, breaking from the bulky wooden enclosures of the time.

Minimalist Form: Clean lines, a monochromatic color scheme, and a flat top design made it visually striking and functionally modern.

User-Friendly Interface: Simple, logical layout with easy-to-use dials and controls, embodying Rams' philosophy of "Less, but better."

Timeless Aesthetic: The SK 4's design language influenced future Braun products and remains an icon of mid-century modernism.

Impact and Legacy

Considered one of the most influential record players of all time. Set the foundation for Braun’s design identity, later inspiring Apple’s product aesthetics.

A prime example of Rams'"10 Principles of Good Design" , particularly honest, useful, and long-lasting design.

Featured in design museums worldwide, including MoMA in New York.
